Database Optimization

  1. Home
  2. Blog Post

Posted on June 7, 2014 at 4:06 PM by Jonathan Young

I would like to preface this blog post by saying that I have been working with SQL and databases for less than 72 hours. While I did do my research in preparing for this blog post and believe that my recommendations are correct, some of the more technical aspects of SQL may be incorrect. That being said…

Let’s say you’re the main marketing guy at Amazon. You have tens of millions of customers. Each customer has a birthday, an email address, a type of payment they use, and different orders. You want a way to organize that so you put them all into a database. At the same time, you have to figure out what you have in stock, what you need to restock, prices, everything! How many databases do you figure you have now? A dozen? More? How many entries are there? A million? Tens of millions? Hundreds of millions? At this point, it doesn’t matter the specifics; we know that it’s a lot of people. Every single query better be optimal because at the rate people order from Amazon, the slightest hiccup could mess up the entire system.

That being said, there are things to do and things not to do to optimize your databases’ performance. Here are some of them:

DO: be specific with what you want the database to search for. Telling the database exactly what you want will cut back on spending unnecessary power.

DO NOT: Use the * symbol to print everything. This results in using more memory than is necessary.

DO: Avoid use IN if you can. IN is the slowest performing function.

DO: Fine tune your database as you go along. Despite having a fast server and more memory than you’ve ever seen, not fine tuning and refactoring your database as you move along will end in disaster.

DO: Backup your databases, lest you mess up.

DO: Use greater/less than OR EQUAL TO instead of just greater/less than. This tells the database exactly where to start looking for something.

DO NOT: Use wildcards (%) like candy. Make sure you have a suffix or prefix to limit the entries you’re sifting through and filter them out. Having a free wildcard is a recipe to slow everything down.

DO NOT: Freely use NOT functions. Just like it’s easier for you to look for something rather than look for everything that something is NOT, it’s the same way with a computer.
